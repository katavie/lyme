Causative Agent

The bacterium Borrelia burgdorferi causes Lyme disease.

Where It Infects

Blacklegged ticks or deer ticks infected with B. burgdorferi bite humans. After 36 to 48 hours, the bacteria enter the bloodstream through the bites.

    Epidemiology

  • 3300 BC A recent autopsy of a 5300-year-old mummy indicated the presence of B. burgdorferi, the bacteria that causes Lyme disease.
  • 1883 Alfred Buchwald, a German physician, described erythema migrans, the chronic skin rashes that we now know is associated with Lyme disease.
  • 1970s Many children and adults in and near the town of Lyme, Connecticut, developed strange symptoms of a disease and went undiagnosed and untreated for years. Two mothers took the initiative to become patient advocates, taking notes and contacting scientists and medical professionals. Research found that all the patients remembered being bitten by a tick, so the disease, whose mechanism was still unknown, was called Lyme.
  • 1980s Willy Burgdofer, a scientist focused on Rocky Mountain Spotted Fever, discovered a link between the deer tick and Lyme disease. He found that bacteria carried by ticks caused the disease, so doctors started to use antibiotics to effectively treat the disease, though they're less effective for late-stage instances of Lyme and may lead to antibiotic resistance over time.
  • 2000s A significant public health concern, Lyme disease has been reported in every US state except Hawaii and is among the fastest-growing vector-borne infections in the country.

    • Lyme disease is the most common tick-borne disease in the Northern hemisphere. Ninety-five percent of confirmed cases in the United States in 2015 were reported from 14 states: CT, DE, ME, MD, MA, MN, NH, NJ, NY, PA, VT, VA, and WI. Infected ticks are also present on the west coast, particularly in northern California, Oregon, and Washington.

Transmission

Lyme disease is contracted through the bite of infected ticks. There is no evidence of it being spread from person-to-person, meat-/pet-to-person, or through other ways.

Mechanism

After entering a human body through the bite of an infected tick, B. burgdorferi evades the host immune system and reaches the central nervous system. It then crosses the blood-brain barrier and enters the cerebrospinal fluid, causing an inflammatory response.

Background pictue shows bacteria (arrows) attached to neurons. This direct interaction could be responsible for the dysfunction that occurs in Lyme disease.

Symptoms

Early symptoms:

Fever, chills, headache, fatigue, muscle and joint aches, swollen lymph nodes, erythema migrans (EM) rash (gradually expanding, warm to touch, not itchy or painful, any area of body)

Later symptoms:

Severe headaches, neck stiffness, pain and swelling in joints, nerve pain, short-term memory problems, dizziness, shortness of breath, irregular heart beat, facial palsy

Treatment

Oral antibiotics, including doxycycline, amoxicillin, or cefuroxime axetil, are use to treat patients in the early stages of Lyme disease, with high success.

Background image shows chemical structure of doxycycline.

Prevention

Lyme disease is prevented by avoiding being bitten by infected ticks.

It is essential to know when and where to expect ticks (grassy, wooded areas during warmer months). Insect repellents and clothing treated with permethrin can help, as well as checking clothing, body parts, gear, and pets after going outside.

The background image shows a man treating his clothing with permethrin, an insecticide that humans are not sensitive to.
